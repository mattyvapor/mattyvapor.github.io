Tuson et al. 2024 — Tracking Deaths of Homeless People Perth

This is a population-based dynamic cohort study tracking deaths among people experiencing homelessness in Perth, Western Australia, between 2020 and 2022. Published in BMJ Open (2024;14:e081260), it is one of the first studies to systematically quantify homeless mortality in WA using linked administrative data.

Method

The cohort was built from multiple administrative datasets — not a survey or self-report sample. Participants were identified through their contact with homelessness services. Death was verified for 93% of cases through two or more data sources, which is a methodological strength: linked data studies of this kind often rely on single-source verification that can miss or misattribute deaths.

The cohort comprised 8,753 individuals. 360 deaths were recorded during the 2020–2022 period.

Key findings

The median age of death in the cohort was 50 years. The median age of death in the Australian general population is 82 years. That is a 32-year gap.

73% of those who died were male. 30% were Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander — against approximately 3.5% Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander in the general WA population.

The study covers 2020–2022, which includes the COVID-19 pandemic period. The authors note this context, though they do not attribute the mortality patterns primarily to COVID-19.

What the numbers mean

A 32-year gap in median age of death is not a marginal finding. It suggests that homelessness in Perth is not a temporary disadvantage that people pass through — for a substantial portion of this cohort, it is a terminal condition. The people dying in this sample are dying in their forties and fifties from conditions that, in the general population, are managed across decades of continuous healthcare.

The Aboriginal overrepresentation is stark and not explained by the study itself — the study identifies it, not its causes. The explanation requires a broader historical and structural account: dispossession, inadequate housing supply in remote and regional WA, discrimination in the public housing system (documented in Finding a Place - EOC Report 2010), and persistent poverty. See Homeless Mortality for more on this.

Significance for policy

The study provides a quantitative anchor for advocacy around homelessness funding in WA. The 2023 parliamentary inquiry (Report 88) and the government's response (see WA Response to Report 88 2023) both drew on evidence of mortality outcomes to justify the scale of investment committed.

The gap between the mortality data and what current policy commitments can realistically address is large. A 32-year gap in life expectancy is not closed by a single government investment cycle, particularly when the supply of housing is the binding constraint and the services needed to support people once housed are separately resourced.

Limitations

The cohort covers only people who accessed formal homelessness services — people who are entirely outside the service system are not captured. This means the 8,753 figure and the death counts are floor estimates for the people experiencing homelessness in Perth over this period. The actual number of people experiencing homelessness, and the actual number of deaths, is likely higher.

Key Takeaways

  • Cohort of 8,753 people experiencing homelessness in Perth, 2020–2022; 360 deaths recorded.
  • Median age of death: 50 years, against 82 years in the general Australian population.
  • 73% of deaths were male; 30% were Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander.
  • 93% of deaths verified via two or more data sources — robust linked-data methodology.
  • Study covers the COVID-19 period but does not attribute the mortality patterns primarily to COVID-19.
  • Cohort is limited to people who accessed formal services — deaths outside the service system are not counted.
