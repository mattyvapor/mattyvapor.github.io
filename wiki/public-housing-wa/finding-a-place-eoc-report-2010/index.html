Finding a Place — EOC Report 2010

Finding a Place is the final report of the Implementation and Monitoring Committee for the Section 80 Inquiry into discriminatory practices in the provision of public housing to Aboriginal people in Western Australia. The EOC released the original inquiry in December 2004, after Acting Commissioner Moira Rayner initiated a formal investigation into the Department of Housing and Works following a wave of discrimination complaints from Aboriginal tenants and applicants between 1998 and 2002. The 2010 document covers six years of implementation tracking — whether the Department actually did what the EOC recommended.

The short version: some things changed. The conditions that produced unequal outcomes mostly did not.

Background

The 2004 inquiry found indirect race discrimination — conditions and requirements that were harder for Aboriginal people to comply with than non-Aboriginal tenants, not through stated intent but through effect. Overcrowding from extended family obligations, limited familiarity with bureaucratic processes, and lower English literacy rates meant that policies designed to be universal were not, in practice, equally accessible.

165 recommendations came out of the 2004 inquiry across five areas: staffing, communication, policy and practices, maintenance and tenancy management, and appeals. The Implementation and Monitoring Committee — government agencies, community organisations, individual Aboriginal community members — met every two months from 2005 to October 2010 to track progress.

Reform Area 1 — Staffing

The Accommodation Manager role was the main structural change. At the time of the inquiry, one position carried both tenancy enforcement and technical maintenance, creating a relationship with tenants built mostly around conflict. The Department split it into two: Housing Services Officers (HSOs) for tenancy support, Property Services Officers (PSOs) for maintenance. HSOs ended up with an average of 350 tenancies each — private sector property managers typically handle 150–200.

Aboriginal staffing in mid-2010: 7.35% of the Department's workforce, against 21.74% of tenants. Staff were concentrated at lower levels — 11.24% at Level 2 (Customer Service Officers) — and nearly absent from management grades. Cultural awareness training was introduced at induction with a four-year refresh cycle. The Committee considered four years too long, the training insufficiently embedded in practice, and the evaluation mechanisms inadequate. These concerns produced Recommendations 4 and 5 in the 2010 report.

The derogatory internal term "Fund 6" used to refer to Aboriginal housing was abolished.

Reform Area 2 — Communication

Standard letters, policy fact sheets, and the Departmental website were rewritten in plain English. A sub-committee of the Monitoring Committee reviewed the core letter suite — property offers, rent arrears notices, breach letters, eviction notices. Colour coding was introduced so tenants could gauge the seriousness of correspondence without reading every word.

Interpreter services were a consistent failure. The correlation between regions with high Aboriginal tenancy and regions where an Aboriginal language is the primary home language is direct: in the Kimberley, 70.84% of Department tenants are Aboriginal, and a substantial proportion don't speak English as a first language. The EOC released a formal report on the need for an Indigenous Interpreter Service in July 2010. During the same period, the Pilbara interpreter service closed.

The Department introduced a centralised 1300 number for maintenance requests. In regional and remote areas this produced two problems: many Aboriginal tenants found ringing an anonymous 1300 number culturally alien compared to face-to-face contact, and for tenants using mobile phones rather than fixed lines, the call was not free. The report speculates — reasonably — that these barriers meant maintenance requests weren't being lodged, and that tenant liability was accruing as a result.

Regional consultation expanded during the period: 176 meetings or open forums at regional/district level between 2008 and 2010, attended by 863 Aboriginal tenants.

Reform Area 3 — Policy and practices

Several concrete policy changes were made over the six years.

Identification requirements were broadened. Letters from community Elders became an accepted form of identification, acknowledging that many Aboriginal people don't hold drivers' licences or passports.

Old debts: statute-barred debts older than six years, where the Department had not previously sought recovery, were quashed. A debt discount scheme was introduced and publicised to current and prospective tenants.

Annual surveys: the old practice of removing applicants from the waiting list for failing to respond was replaced with a three-contact requirement before any removal. Regional advocates still reported in 2010 that some offices weren't following the new process.

Private rental: the requirement for Aboriginal applicants to prove attempts at the private rental market before being considered for priority housing was abolished. A 2009 EOC inquiry into private rental had confirmed that race discrimination was occurring; requiring Aboriginal people to demonstrate repeated rejection before accessing public housing was both ineffective and humiliating.

Secondary homelessness was a significant unresolved issue. The Rental Policy Manual still distinguished between primary homelessness and secondary homelessness (staying with family or friends), with the latter requiring proof that the arrangement couldn't continue before priority assistance would be granted. The report describes the structural problem plainly: Aboriginal families take in relatives as a cultural obligation. This produces overcrowding in existing tenancies, which in turn produces tenant liability charges, anti-social behaviour complaints from neighbours, and increased maintenance demands. The 2010 report recommended removing the primary/secondary distinction from policy altogether.

The priority housing waiting list grew significantly during the period. In 2004, around 700 people were on the priority list, with a wait of four to six months. By 2010, there were over 3,000 applicants, with average wait times exceeding two years. Around 40% of priority allocations between 2005 and 2008 went to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander clients. By December 2010, 80% of all Department allocations were coming from the priority list rather than the wait-turn list.

Reform Area 4 — Maintenance and tenancy management

A maintenance review completed in 2009 formalised response timeframes: emergency within 3 hours, priority within 48 hours, routine within 14 days. Completed work on jobs over $400 required PSO sign-off. A centralised phone registration system was introduced.

Evictions remained disproportionate. Bailiff evictions by year, against Aboriginal tenants' 21.74% share of the tenant population:

Year ATSI share of bailiff evictions
2006 35%
2007 49%
2008 47%
2009 49%
2010 (to June) 63%

The primary driver of terminations was rental arrears. An AHURI study (Beer, 2006) found that only 7% of evictions resulted from bailiff or police action — most tenants left after receiving a termination notice or in anticipation of one. In the first half of 2010, 1,518 termination notices were issued across the state. The Caretaker database couldn't break these down by reason; the figures had to be calculated manually.

Anti-social behaviour procedures were revised: the definition was narrowed to exclude trivial complaints (children running across lawns, noise a private landlord wouldn't normally act on). Tenants were entitled to be informed of allegations and given the opportunity to respond. The Disruptive Behaviour Management Strategy, announced by the Minister in late 2009, attracted formal concern from the EOC about children's rights under the Convention on the Rights of the Child, the Department's role as housing of last resort, domestic violence situations where the behaviour being complained about might involve the perpetrator, and cases where anti-social behaviour was attributable to intellectual disability or mental illness. The Committee endorsed the EOC's submission on each of these points.

Reform Area 5 — Appeals

The Housing Appeals Mechanism was restructured in September 2009. The third tier — a three-person external panel — was removed in favour of a two-tier system: senior officer review followed by a regional panel with two independent members. The Committee obtained assurances that panel members were not former Departmental employees and that training on equal opportunity law would be provided.

The remaining gap was the exclusion of termination notices from the list of appealable decisions. A tenant could appeal a tenant liability charge or a transfer decision, but not the decision to issue a notice to vacate. Recommendation 9 of the 2010 report sought to change this.

Emerging issues

Emergency housing had moved out of the Department's remit to the Department for Child Protection. The Department retained the Homeless Advisory Service (a daily-updated register of crisis accommodation), but no longer had a direct emergency housing function.

The priority waitlist was producing an increasing volume of complaints to the EOC — people whose circumstances (chronic illness, domestic violence, children at risk) had not been adequately weighted in a date-ordered system. The Commission was advocating for input into the design of any housing needs register that might replace it.

Community housing was being given greater responsibility for stock previously managed by the Department, partly as a response to Commonwealth-State funding pressures. The Committee noted that the policy reforms achieved over six years could be undermined if community housing contracts did not require equivalent standards.

From 1 July 2010, the Aboriginal Housing Legislation Amendment Act 2010 extended the Department's responsibilities to housing management in remote Aboriginal communities, under a framework of Housing Management Agreements. The scope extension was substantial. The Committee noted the need to ensure rent collection, asset protection, tenant support, and governance processes in remote communities met the standards built in the urban and regional context over the previous six years.

New recommendations (2010)

The 2010 report made 12 new recommendations:

  1. Embed a cultural awareness criterion in the role statement of all frontline and management positions, with annual performance review component.
  2. Develop strategies to recruit, retain, and promote Aboriginal staff at all levels.
  3. Commission an independent confidential survey of Aboriginal staff covering satisfaction, perceived support, and experiences of discrimination.
  4. Review cultural awareness training intervals and embed awareness in operational activities rather than induction only.
  5. Commission an independent survey of Aboriginal tenants and applicants on whether staff interactions had improved.
  6. Amend the Rental Policy Manual to remove the distinction between primary and secondary homelessness in priority assistance eligibility.
  7. Develop systems to record termination notices, vacation rates, and court applications by reason, region, and Aboriginal/non-Aboriginal status.
  8. Make analysis of policies and practices leading to eviction an ongoing priority area between the Department and the EOC.
  9. Amend the Housing Appeals Mechanism to include termination notices as an appealable decision.
  10. Include the Commissioner for Equal Opportunity in any process to revise waitlist criteria or design a housing needs register.
  11. Ensure the Disruptive Behaviour Management Strategy accounts for the concerns in the EOC's February 2010 submission.
  12. Establish an electronic data system capable of producing regular reporting on inequalities in housing outcomes.

Five monitoring points required regional offices to report quarterly on Aboriginal interpreter use and unmet need; the Department to report half-yearly on priority waitlist wait times by region and dwelling type; HSO average tenancy loads, staff training participation rates, and policy fact sheets to be tracked and conveyed to the EOC as produced.

Implementation status

Appendix 2 catalogues all 165 original recommendations. The dominant status categories are "Recommendation Supported" (agreed but not done), "Not Currently Addressed," and "Partially Implemented." Fully implemented recommendations were a minority, concentrated in structural governance changes: establishing the committee, abolishing the Fund 6 designation, adjusting some aspects of the application process. Structural changes to housing stock, staffing levels, data collection, and eviction processes mostly did not make it into the implemented column.

What changed, what didn't

The Department restructured frontline roles, rewrote letters, introduced cultural awareness training, relaxed identification requirements, abolished some discriminatory practices, and tightened the anti-social behaviour framework. The Committee's work produced a more granular picture of how Departmental policy operated in practice against Aboriginal tenants.

But eviction rates stayed disproportionate through to 2010, and the final year of data showed them getting worse, not better. The interpreter service in the Pilbara closed. The priority waiting list went from 700 to 3,000 applicants and the average wait time blew out from months to years. The data systems needed to measure inequality didn't exist. Community housing expansion raised real questions about whether the protections built over six years would carry across.

Six-monthly forums, broadened to include more advocates and housing groups, were established as the ongoing accountability mechanism after the Committee formally ceased meeting in October 2010.

Key Takeaways

  • Finding a Place (2010) is the monitoring report on a 2004 EOC inquiry into discriminatory public housing practices affecting Aboriginal people in WA.
  • Five reform areas: staffing, communication, policy and practices, maintenance and tenancy management, appeals.
  • Procedural changes were made; structural conditions producing disproportionate outcomes persisted.
  • Eviction rates for Aboriginal tenants remained disproportionate (35–63% of annual bailiff evictions) against 21.74% of the tenant population — and rose through the period.
  • Priority housing waitlist grew from ~700 applicants with a 4–6 month wait in 2004 to 3,000+ applicants with a 2+ year wait by 2010.
  • Aboriginal staffing was 7.35% of the Department's workforce against 21.74% of tenants, concentrated at junior levels.
  • The Pilbara interpreter service closed during the reporting period.
  • The Aboriginal Housing Legislation Amendment Act 2010 extended Department management to remote communities from July 2010.
  • 12 new recommendations and 5 monitoring points were made in the 2010 report; most original 165 recommendations remained unsatisfied.
