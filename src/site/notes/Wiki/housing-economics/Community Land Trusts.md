# Community Land Trusts

A Community Land Trust (CLT) is a nonprofit organisation that owns land permanently on behalf of a community, leasing it to households at below-market rates. Residents own their dwellings but not the land underneath. When they sell, a resale formula limits the profit they can capture — typically linking their gain to inflation or income growth rather than the full speculative market price. This is how the affordability is locked in: each resale passes the home on at below-market price to the next buyer.

The model trades speculative gain for permanent affordability. For households who want stable ownership without gambling on land prices, it's a reasonable trade.

## International examples

Murray (2018) cites several examples to establish that CLTs work at scale:

Champlain Housing Trust in Burlington, Vermont, is one of the oldest and largest. Founded in 1984, it has grown to over 2,200 permanently affordable dwellings. Prices sit at around 26% below market rate on resale.

San Francisco has a smaller CLT with over 800 units, with resale prices approximately 47% below comparable market properties.

As of Murray's writing, there were around 250 CLTs operating across the United States. In the UK, the sector effectively didn't exist before the 2008 global financial crisis; by 2018 there were around 280, reflecting a decade of rapid growth driven partly by post-GFC pressure on social housing and private rental.

## CLTs in Australia

The CLT model has been slow to develop in Australia. Murray (2018) attributes this partly to the political economy of housing: the mechanisms that dominate Australian housing policy (negative gearing, capital gains discounts, first home buyer grants) disproportionately benefit existing landholders and incentivise speculative ownership rather than stable, affordable tenure. CLTs are structurally at odds with this.

There have been CLT pilots in Australia, including in Western Australia, but the sector is not at the scale seen in the US or UK.

## How CLTs differ from public housing and private ownership

Public housing provides secure tenure but depends on ongoing government funding and carries the political risk of asset sales. Private ownership creates equity but exposes households to land price speculation and prices people out of stable communities when markets heat up. CLTs sit between these: permanent tenure security, below-market prices, and community governance, but without the state-dependency of public housing and without the speculative exposure of private ownership.

This positions them as a potential complement to [[Wiki/public-housing-wa/_index\|public housing]] rather than a replacement — serving households whose incomes are too high for public housing but too low for the private market in high-price areas.

For the land economics argument underlying CLTs, see [[Wiki/housing-economics/Land Rent Scheme\|Land Rent Scheme]] and [[Wiki/housing-economics/Murray 2018 - Unspoken Alternatives to Expensive Housing\|Murray 2018 - Unspoken Alternatives to Expensive Housing]].

## Key Takeaways

- CLTs own land permanently through a nonprofit, leasing it to households who own their dwellings.
- Resale formulas limit profit capture and lock in affordability across generations.
- Major examples: Champlain Housing Trust (2,200+ dwellings, 26% below market); San Francisco CLT (800+ dwellings, 47% below market); ~250 US CLTs; UK sector grew from ~2 to ~280 in the decade after 2008.
- CLTs are underdeveloped in Australia, partly because they cut against the land-speculative incentives dominating Australian housing policy.
- Positioned as a complement to public housing, not a replacement — targeting moderate-income households priced out of the private market.
