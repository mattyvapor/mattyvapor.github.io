# Land Rent Scheme

A land rent scheme separates ownership of land from ownership of the dwelling built on it. Buyers purchase only the house; the land is leased from a public or community body at an annual rent. Because land represents a significant portion of property prices in Australian cities, removing it from the purchase price substantially lowers the cost of entry into homeownership.

## The ACT Land Rent Scheme

The most substantive Australian example is the ACT Land Rent Scheme (LRS), which Murray (2018) analyses in some detail. As of the time of Murray's report, 3,268 households were participating in the scheme. Over the life of those arrangements, participants had saved a combined $69 million compared with full freehold purchase — around $9 million per year.

The ACT scheme charges an annual land rent (the ACT government retains ownership of the land). Participants can opt out and convert to full freehold purchase, though the scheme is designed to make the rental path financially attractive enough that many don't. The land rent rate is set by formula rather than market — another way of insulating participants from speculative land price movements.

## Why land value matters

Murray (2018) frames Australian housing affordability problems as fundamentally about land, not buildings. Buildings depreciate; land appreciates. The mechanisms that push up house prices — first home buyer grants, stamp duty concessions, negative gearing, developer charges, rezoning capital gains — all end up capitalised into land values. The beneficiaries are landholders, not people trying to get into housing.

A land rent scheme breaks this by removing the land component from the transaction. Participants don't pay inflated land prices upfront. They don't benefit from speculative land gains later, but they also don't carry speculative land risk. The dwelling functions more like other consumer goods: it costs what it costs to build and maintain.

## Limits

The ACT has a relatively unusual tenure history — the Crown retained land ownership under the original 1911 Lease framework in ways that made the land rent scheme administratively feasible. Replicating this in WA or other states would require either large-scale land acquisition by government or alternative arrangements where government leases back land it already holds (e.g., former public housing land).

For an alternative community-based model, see [[Wiki/housing-economics/Community Land Trusts\|Community Land Trusts]], which achieves a similar affordability outcome through nonprofit land ownership rather than government land ownership.

## Key Takeaways

- Land rent schemes separate land ownership from dwelling ownership, reducing purchase prices by removing the land component.
- The ACT Land Rent Scheme had 3,268 participants generating $69 million in cumulative savings (~$9M/year) at the time of Murray (2018).
- Murray's argument is that Australian housing prices are fundamentally a land value problem — the mechanisms that subsidise homeownership mostly flow to landholders.
- Replication in other states faces structural barriers around land ownership that the ACT's unusual tenure history doesn't share.
