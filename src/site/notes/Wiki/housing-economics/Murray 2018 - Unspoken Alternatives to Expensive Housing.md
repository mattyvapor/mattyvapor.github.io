# Murray 2018 — Unspoken Alternatives to Expensive Housing

Published by Prosper Australia and the Australia Institute, this report argues that Australian housing policy systematically avoids the most effective interventions because those interventions would harm existing landholders. The alternatives Murray calls "unspoken" — land rent schemes and community land trusts — are not untested or theoretical. They are well-evidenced and operating at scale internationally. The reason they are absent from mainstream Australian policy debate, Murray argues, is political rather than practical.

## The core argument

Australian housing prices are, at their root, land prices. This matters because land and buildings have completely different economics. Buildings are constructed goods that depreciate over time. Land is a location — finite, non-reproducible in the ways that matter, and appreciating over time as cities grow and infrastructure improves.

The policy instruments that dominate Australian housing debate — first home buyer grants, stamp duty concessions, negative gearing, capital gains tax discounts, developer levies — all interact with land prices in ways that benefit existing landholders. First home buyer grants, for example, increase purchasing capacity without increasing supply, which drives land prices up and hands the grant value directly to sellers. Negative gearing and capital gains discounts incentivise speculative investment in land-heavy assets. Rezoning decisions create windfall gains for landholders who didn't create the value being captured.

Murray's framing draws on the classical political economy distinction between land, labour, and capital. Land value is socially created (by infrastructure, by agglomeration, by population growth) but privately captured. This is the monopoly that produces unaffordable housing, and it is what land rent schemes and CLTs are designed to interrupt.

## What Murray proposes

The report makes the case for two models, both with substantial evidence bases:

[[Wiki/housing-economics/Land Rent Scheme\|Land Rent Scheme]]: the ACT Land Rent Scheme had 3,268 participants at the time of writing, with $69 million in cumulative savings (~$9M/year). The scheme separates land from dwelling at point of purchase, leasing the land from the Crown at an annual rate. Participants get homeownership without the land price premium.

[[Wiki/housing-economics/Community Land Trusts\|Community Land Trusts]]: Murray cites the Champlain Housing Trust (Burlington, Vermont, founded 1984, 2,200+ dwellings, 26% below market), San Francisco CLT (800+ units, 47% below market), and the broader US (~250 CLTs) and UK (~280 CLTs by 2018, up from ~2 before the GFC) sectors. CLTs achieve permanent affordability through nonprofit land ownership and resale price formulas.

## Why these alternatives are "unspoken"

Murray is direct about this: the political economy of Australian housing is structured around landholders. Both major parties have large constituencies of existing homeowners whose primary financial asset is their home. Policies that would moderate or reduce land prices are politically difficult to advocate, regardless of their merits for people trying to get into housing.

This is not primarily a policy design problem. The designs exist and work. It is a problem of who benefits from the status quo and how much political power they hold.

## What's missing from the report

Murray's argument is strongest on the economics and the international evidence. It is less developed on how to build the political conditions for adoption — the report is a case for the policies, not a theory of change for getting them implemented.

It is also worth noting that land rent and CLT models solve affordability at point of entry and over the lifetime of a household. They don't directly address the most acute housing need — the people experiencing homelessness or in crisis accommodation who need housing now. For that, see [[Wiki/homelessness-wa/_index\|Homelessness in WA]].

## Key Takeaways

- Australian housing unaffordability is primarily a land price problem, not a building cost problem.
- Policy instruments like FHOG, negative gearing, and capital gains discounts transfer value to existing landholders rather than improving access.
- Land rent schemes (ACT: 3,268 households, $69M in savings) and CLTs (250+ in US, 280 in UK) are the alternatives Murray presents.
- The alternatives are "unspoken" for political reasons, not practical ones — they are well-evidenced internationally.
- Murray's report makes the case for the policy; it doesn't provide a theory of political change.
