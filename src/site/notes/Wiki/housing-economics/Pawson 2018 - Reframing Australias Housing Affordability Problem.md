# Pawson 2018 — Reframing Australia's Housing Affordability Problem

Published in the *Journal of Australian Political Economy* (No. 81, pp. 121–43), this article by Isla Pawson challenges the dominant explanation for why negative gearing survives in Australian policy. The standard account — that it persists because of a "market-based" policy paradigm or bureaucratic inertia — is, Pawson argues, too thin. It treats the state as either captured by bad ideas or simply obstinate. The political economy account is harder to dismiss: negative gearing survives because it serves real material interests, and those interests have structural power.

## The dominant narrative and its problems

Most housing scholarship frames negative gearing's durability as a policy failure — governments have been captured by a "neoliberal paradigm" that privileges market-based welfare over effective intervention. Scholars like Nicholls (2014) and Dodson (2006) trace this to the dominance of pro-market ideas within state institutions.

Pawson identifies two problems with this. First, it treats neoliberalism as an autonomous intellectual force rather than as a set of political practices emerging from material conditions. Second, it implies the state has a coherent and consistent housing policy approach, when in reality housing outcomes are shaped by taxation, welfare, finance, and planning decisions that operate across different policy domains with no unified logic. As Madden and Marcuse (2016) put it: "a historical analysis of government actions and inactions affecting housing reveals nothing of the sort" as a consistent effort to solve the housing problem.

## The political economy alternative

Pawson draws on Harvey, Jessop, Peck, and Madden and Marcuse to build a structural account. The core argument has three moves.

Housing is a "spatial fix" to capital accumulation problems. As Harvey (1982) argues, when surplus capital can't find productive outlets, it flows into property — the construction of built environments becomes a way of temporarily resolving accumulation crises. Australia's housing market is deeply embedded in this logic; property is one of the economy's largest contributors to GDP, and speculative investment in housing is structurally important to Australia's current growth regime.

The state is bound to reproduce the conditions for capital accumulation. Jessop's (2002) state theory holds that capitalist social relations require something outside themselves to reproduce — the state mediates the antagonism between labour and capital and provides the conditions (education, health, housing) that capital can't produce itself. Peck (1996) extends this: the state must "make workers", including providing housing that keeps a workforce in production. Public housing is one form of this, but so are the tax settings that enable private housing investment.

Housing has undergone "hyper-commodification". Madden and Marcuse (2016) argue that housing's function as real estate now takes precedence over its function as shelter — investment value crowds out use value. Pawson applies this to Australia's context of rising prices, wage stagnation, and growing inequality, arguing that the accumulation of wealth through housing is now structurally important to the economy in a way that makes reform politically dangerous.

## The numbers

Yates (2010) estimated total annual housing tax expenditures at around $53 billion. Of that, $45 billion flows to existing homeowners as imputed subsidies. Rental investors receive around $5 billion. Renters — the people who most need housing support — receive $3.2 billion.

Negative gearing specifically cost the federal budget $3.346 billion in 2013–14, roughly equivalent to total spending on jobseeker assistance and vocational training (Martin 2016).

The distributional skew within negative gearing is sharp. Coalition ministers argued that "two thirds" of negative gearers earned under $80,000 a year — a figure that sounds progressive until you note that 82% of all taxpayers are in that bracket. More informative: 29% of surgeons and anaesthetists used negative gearing in 2013–14, compared with 9% of nurses and 12% of teachers. Surgeons received an average benefit of over $3,000; nurses and teachers received under $300 (Daley and Wood 2016).

## Supply didn't fix it

Between 2012 and 2016, Australian dwelling starts rose from 153,000 to 228,000 per year — a 50% increase. Australia was building more dwellings per capita than the US or UK. Over the same period, national median house prices rose 10.6% in 2016 alone (15.5% in Sydney, 13.7% in Melbourne). Rents rose over 48% nationally since the GFC.

Pawson's point is not that supply doesn't matter. It's that supply-side policy served two functions simultaneously: it gave the property development industry a social justification (they were solving the affordability problem) and it allowed the government to appear responsive to the crisis while leaving the demand-side drivers — negative gearing, capital gains discounts — intact.

## Why reform is structurally difficult

Pawson ends with what Phibbs (2017) called "placebo policies" — the 2017 Budget housing package that ostensibly addressed affordability without meaningfully touching demand. This is what you'd expect from a state that is structurally reliant on property as an economic growth engine and politically reliant on the homeowner constituency and property industry donors. It's not primarily a failure of policy ideas. It's a failure that reflects who benefits from the current system and what power they hold.

The article makes no predictions about whether reform will happen. It does argue that understanding *why* negative gearing is durable — not just that it is — is a prerequisite for any serious challenge to it.

## Key Takeaways

- Negative gearing's durability isn't explained by bad ideas or paradigmatic inertia — it's explained by structural political economy: capital accumulation, property industry interests, and the state's dependence on housing as a growth engine.
- Total housing tax expenditure ~$53B/year (Yates 2010): $45B to homeowners, $5B to investors, $3.2B to renters.
- Negative gearing cost $3.346B in 2013–14 (Martin 2016) — redistributing upward, not down.
- Supply increased 50% between 2012 and 2016; prices kept rising. Supply-side policy served investor interests, not affordability.
- "Placebo policies" (Phibbs 2017): government appeared responsive to the housing crisis while preserving the demand-side settings that drive it.
- Draws on Harvey (spatial fix), Jessop (capitalist state), Peck (making workers), Madden and Marcuse (hyper-commodification).
