# Housing First

Housing First is a homelessness intervention model built on a simple reversal of the conventional approach: give people a stable home first, then address whatever else is going on. The traditional approach — often called the "treatment first" or "staircase" model — required people to demonstrate sobriety, mental health stability, or compliance with support programmes before they could access permanent housing. Housing First treats stable housing as a precondition for recovery, not a reward for it.

The model originated with Sam Tsemberis and Pathways to Housing in New York in the 1990s. It has since been implemented across North America, Europe, and Australia, with a growing evidence base showing that it reduces chronic homelessness more effectively than staircase alternatives.

## Housing First in WA

The WA Government's *All Paths Lead to Home* strategy (2020–2030) incorporates Housing First principles as a central plank of its homelessness response. The government has established several Housing First–style programmes as part of a $2.6 billion investment in social housing and homelessness services across four years.

Specific initiatives include:

*Djuripiny Mia* is a 100-home social housing development with wraparound support services. The name is Noongar for "place of healing" and the development is explicitly designed for people with complex needs.

The *Common Ground* model — permanent supportive housing with on-site services — has been implemented in East Perth and Mandurah. Common Ground combines affordable housing for a mixed population (around half tenants from the social housing waiting list, half from street homelessness) with 24/7 support staff.

The *Housing First Homelessness Initiative* targets rough sleepers and those in crisis accommodation with assertive outreach, immediate housing placement, and voluntary support services.

## Limits and tensions

Housing First doesn't eliminate the need for support services — it changes the sequencing. People housed through Housing First still need access to mental health care, drug and alcohol services, income support, and social connection. The question is whether those services are available once someone is housed, which depends on resourcing that Housing First funding doesn't necessarily cover.

There is also a supply constraint that Housing First cannot resolve on its own. The model works best when there are enough dwellings to house people quickly. In WA, where the public housing waiting list is long and the private rental market has historically low vacancy rates, the "housing" part of Housing First requires serious supply expansion — not just a reallocation of what exists.

For WA's current policy commitments on supply, see [[Wiki/homelessness-wa/WA Response to Report 88 2023\|WA Response to Report 88 2023]]. For the mortality context that makes this urgent, see [[Wiki/homelessness-wa/Homeless Mortality\|Homeless Mortality]] and [[Wiki/homelessness-wa/Tuson et al. 2024 - Tracking Deaths of Homeless People Perth\|Tuson et al. 2024 - Tracking Deaths of Homeless People Perth]].

## Key Takeaways

- Housing First prioritises stable housing before treatment or behavioural compliance — the opposite of the staircase model.
- WA's *All Paths Lead to Home* (2020–2030) adopts Housing First as a framework.
- Djuripiny Mia (100 homes), Common Ground (East Perth and Mandurah), and the Housing First Homelessness Initiative are the main WA implementations.
- Housing First doesn't resolve the underlying supply shortage or the services gap once people are housed.
