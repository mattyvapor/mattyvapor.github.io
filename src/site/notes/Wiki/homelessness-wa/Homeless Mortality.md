# Homeless Mortality

People experiencing homelessness die significantly earlier than the general population. The causes are partly direct — exposure, violence, overdose — but the bulk of the gap comes from chronic disease and a healthcare system that effectively excludes people without stable housing. In Perth, a 2024 cohort study found a median age of death of 50 years, against 82 years for the general Australian population.

## What the data shows

[[Wiki/homelessness-wa/Tuson et al. 2024 - Tracking Deaths of Homeless People Perth\|Tuson et al. 2024 - Tracking Deaths of Homeless People Perth]] tracked a cohort of 8,753 people who accessed homelessness services in Perth between 2020 and 2022. 360 died during the study period — 73% male, 30% Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander. That last figure is striking: Aboriginal people are around 3.5% of the general WA population but 30% of deaths in this cohort.

Cause-of-death data from the study hasn't been fully extracted here, but the pattern in comparable studies is consistent: cardiovascular disease, chronic liver disease, cancer, and drug- and alcohol-related deaths dominate, along with external causes (suicide, injury, assault). The common thread is that all of these are conditions that benefit from stable housing, continuous medical care, and reliable access to food, warmth, and sanitation.

## The Aboriginal dimension

The overrepresentation of Aboriginal people in homeless mortality data in WA isn't incidental. It reflects the compounding of structural disadvantage: historical dispossession, disproportionate poverty, inadequate housing supply in remote and regional areas, and documented discrimination in the public housing system (see [[Wiki/public-housing-wa/Finding a Place - EOC Report 2010\|Finding a Place - EOC Report 2010]]). A person who is Aboriginal, homeless, and living in WA faces a system that has historically failed them at multiple levels simultaneously.

## Why housing matters for mortality

The relationship between housing and health is not subtle. Stable housing enables consistent access to healthcare. It allows chronic conditions to be managed rather than presenting at crisis point in emergency departments. It provides the physical conditions — warmth, sleep, nutrition — that determine baseline health. Without it, the healthcare system becomes an episodic emergency service rather than a system capable of managing long-term health.

This is part of the evidence base for [[Wiki/homelessness-wa/Housing First\|Housing First]] — the argument that you cannot meaningfully improve health outcomes for people experiencing homelessness without first addressing the housing instability that prevents health improvement.

## Policy response

The mortality picture has been one of the drivers of pressure on WA Government to expand its homelessness response. The 2023 parliamentary inquiry (Report 88) and the government's formal response (see [[Wiki/homelessness-wa/WA Response to Report 88 2023\|WA Response to Report 88 2023]]) both acknowledge the urgency. Whether the current policy commitments — $2.6 billion over four years, 4,000 new social homes, expanded services — are adequate to shift these outcomes is a separate question.

## Key Takeaways

- People experiencing homelessness in Perth have a median age of death of 50 years, against 82 for the general population (Tuson et al., 2024).
- 30% of deaths in the Perth cohort were Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander, against ~3.5% in the general WA population.
- Deaths are driven by chronic disease, substance-related conditions, and external causes — all conditions influenced by housing stability.
- The Aboriginal overrepresentation reflects compounding structural disadvantage, including documented discrimination in public housing access.
- Housing First is the main policy framework for addressing this, but supply constraints limit its reach.
