# Workers Homes Act 1911

The *Workers' Homes Act 1911* (No. 8 of 1912) established the Workers' Homes Board in Western Australia. Despite its 1911 title, the Act was gazetted in 1912 — a minor piece of timing that Sharp (1993) notes is a minor source of confusion in the historical record.

The Act was not a social housing programme in any modern sense. It was a homeownership assistance scheme, targeted at working men with modest incomes. The state's interest was in settling a mobile, resource-dependent workforce.

## Who it covered

Eligibility was income-tested. Workers earning up to £300 per year could apply. This excluded the genuinely poor — people below consistent wage employment — and also the more prosperous. It was designed specifically for the respectable working class: regularly employed, financially manageable, family-oriented.

## What it offered

The Act provided two mechanisms. Workers could lease land from the Crown on a perpetual lease at 3% per annum. They could also take out a state-backed advance for a dwelling, up to £550, repayable over 30 years at 5% per annum. If repayments were made punctually, the interest rate dropped to 5.5% — an early version of discount-for-compliance logic.

Advances were secured by mortgage at 6% nominal (5.5% with the rebate), which the Board held over the property. Workers built or bought their homes; the state held security and received repayments. The transaction was closer to a building society than a housing charity.

## The Workers' Homes Board

The Board administered the scheme. Sharp (1993) describes it as operating under the overlapping pressures of workforce housing demand from industry, housing demand from low-income earners, and the state's broader interest in population growth and economic development. The Board was not insulated from these pressures — it responded to them, adjusting loan terms and eligibility as conditions changed.

The physical size and climatic variation of WA also shaped the Board's operations. Housing in the north required different materials and construction approaches than in Perth. Decentralised administration was a practical necessity, not an ideological choice.

## What it wasn't

The Act didn't offer public rental housing. It didn't serve Aboriginal Western Australians. It didn't cover agricultural workers or domestic workers. The workers it assisted were already in a position to sustain regular loan repayments — the most marginalised were excluded from the outset.

Sharp (1993) uses this to anchor a larger argument about housing as social control: the scheme rewarded respectable workforce participation and homeownership as a form of social settlement. Access to it was a privilege extended to productive citizens, not a right granted to residents.

## Legacy

The Workers' Homes Board operated from 1912 until it was replaced by the State Housing Commission after World War II. The shift reflected changed conditions — postwar housing shortage, mass migration, and a political environment in which public rental housing had become viable at scale. But the underlying logic the Board had established — that housing access should be earned, managed, and conditional — persisted in how the Commission operated.

## Key Takeaways

- The Workers' Homes Act 1911 (WA) created a homeownership loan scheme, not a social housing programme.
- Eligibility was restricted to workers earning ≤ £300/year — it excluded both the very poor and the prosperous.
- Land was available on perpetual lease at 3% p.a.; dwelling advances up to £550 at 5% over 30 years.
- The scheme was designed for workforce settlement, not welfare.
- Sharp (1993) reads it as an early instance of housing-as-social-control: conditional access tied to respectability and productive employment.
