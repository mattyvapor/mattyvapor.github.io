# Sharp 1993 — A History of Public Housing in Western Australia

Sharp's 1993 thesis examines public housing in WA through two organisations: the Workers' Homes Board (established 1912) and the State Housing Commission (post-WWII, trading as Homeswest from 1985). It is primarily a history of what shaped these organisations rather than a standard institutional account — Sharp is more interested in the forces acting on the system than in the bureaucratic detail.

## Central argument

The thesis makes a specific claim worth holding onto: public housing in WA functioned as an instrument of social control. Access was structured as a reward — for workforce participation, for respectable household behaviour, for financial reliability — rather than as a right conferred by need or residence. Sharp doesn't frame this as a conspiracy. It was the operating logic of the system, embedded in eligibility criteria, allocation decisions, and tenancy management.

That said, Sharp doesn't reduce the history to top-down control. There is also evidence throughout the thesis that tenant agency and community values shaped what the organisations actually did. Clients pushed back. Communities organised. Policy responded, sometimes. The relationship between the state and its housing clients was not simply one of control and compliance.

## Three driving pressures

Sharp identifies three overlapping factors that shaped how both organisations operated:

Industry demand for workforce housing. WA's economy was built on resource extraction and agriculture, both of which required mobile, temporary, or regional labour. Housing was a practical necessity for attracting and retaining workers. The state provided it because industry needed it.

Demand from low-income earners. There was persistent unmet demand for affordable housing from people who couldn't access the private market. Both organisations responded to this, though housing for low-income earners was always secondary to the workforce and economic development priorities.

The state's interest in population and economic growth. WA was chronically underpopulated relative to its land area and resource potential. Public housing was part of how the state attracted and settled migrants, particularly in the postwar period when migration programmes brought large numbers of Europeans to Perth and regional centres.

## Material factors

Sharp also gives weight to things that don't fit neatly into a political economy argument. The physical size of WA, its climatic variation, and the material constraints of the postwar period all shaped what was built, where, and for whom. Decentralisation wasn't just a policy choice — it was a necessity in a state where major regional centres are several hundred kilometres from Perth.

## Conclusion

Sharp's overall assessment of the state's role is positive, cautiously. Public housing in WA contributed to economic growth, provided infrastructure for industry, housed postwar migrants, and gave affordable accommodation to low-income families. It did real things. But it also controlled, excluded, and rationed — and the thesis is honest about that.

The tension Sharp identifies between housing as social control and housing as social provision runs through the entire history. It doesn't resolve.

## Key Takeaways

- Sharp frames WA public housing as shaped by workforce demand, low-income need, and population/economic growth — not primarily by welfare intent.
- The central thesis is that housing access functioned as social control: a conditional reward rather than a right.
- Tenant agency and community values also shaped the system — it wasn't purely top-down.
- Sharp's assessment of the state's role is net positive, while acknowledging the exclusions.
- The Workers' Homes Board (1912) and State Housing Commission (post-WWII) are treated as a single continuous story.
